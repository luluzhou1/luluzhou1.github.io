This blog post is the result of an interview with [Cody Littley](https://github.com/cody-littley), an excellent programmer and my colleague at Eigen Labs, about how to grow as a junior programmer in the era of AI.

## Complexity
Complexity is bad [1]. More specifically, *creating* complexity is bad. Reducing complexity is good. Reducing complexity means understanding and digesting a complex problem, then coming up with a simple solution. The value of a programmer’s job (and many other jobs) is to *deal with* complexity.

## Two Parts of a Programmer’s Job
Let’s break a programmer’s job into two parts.

The less complex part is implementing a function. You use algorithms and data structures to write code that performs a specific task with defined inputs and outputs. A good example is LeetCode-style problems. AI can usually do this job very well.

However, the fact that AI can do this well does not mean CS students or junior programmers no longer need to learn it. To be a good programmer, it is still necessary to be able to write code without relying on AI. Understanding how things work under the hood is important for debugging and handling emergencies. This part also teaches fundamental programming ideas that are used everywhere and helps you understand how computers work.

The speed and accuracy of solving these problems may not be as important as before now that we have AI. It’s unlikely that the next generation of programmers will spend time solving more than 1,000 LeetCode exercises to pass interviews. Still, it is valuable to spend time on coding basics and be comfortable writing code by hand, even when AI is available.

One challenge is that almost all CS course homework in college can now be solved by AI. It is very tempting for students to get easy answers. It may be worth creating learning environments where AI is not allowed to give direct answers, but instead works like a TA by giving hints and feedback while students learn how to code.

The more complex part of a programmer’s job is solving real-world problems: understanding real needs, designing the architecture, and implementing solutions in a readable and maintainable way. AI is not particularly good at this. It often comes up with solutions that are unnecessarily complex. Human experience matters a lot here, and the way to get better is by doing the work, reflecting on your solutions, and finding better ways to solve problems.

In fact, the time saved by AI on the first part allows programmers to spend more time on this second part. I believe this is the real value of a programmer’s job. The ability to handle complex problems and solve them with simple code is what differentiates mediocre programmers from good ones.

## Can AI Deal With All the Complexity of a Programmer’s Job?
AI seems to have the potential to handle a lot of complexity in programming. However, as mentioned above, current AI is not good enough to produce clean, secure, and simple end-to-end solutions for real-world needs or to make good architectural decisions.

This limitation may even be fundamental, since AI is essentially a word-guessing system. For now, senior programmers’ jobs are relatively safe, and AI can be a powerful tool that helps them work much faster. For junior programmers, the situation is tougher.

Still, I believe in the long-term value of learning the basics and accumulating experience in designing, building, and maintaining code over time. Programming is likely to remain a job that requires dealing with complexity that AI cannot fully handle, making it a meaningful and valuable career to spend your life on.
